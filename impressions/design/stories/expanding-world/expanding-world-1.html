Chapter 1

The weather was mild enough in early March 1835 that Greenfield, Massachusetts, a town of 1,500 souls tucked into the Connecticut River valley, was busy laying new sidewalks. Dexter Marsh, the town’s handyman, was 29 years old, strong and vigorous. He had quarried sandstone from nearby Montague and used oxen or horses to haul it to Greenfield’s Clay Hill, where Town Hall presided and, incidentally, he had built his own small home. He probably was prying a slab apart when he noticed what appeared to be footprints pressed into one side and the same exact shapes raised up on the facing slab he had just pulled away.

Dexter probably knew a little about rocks and fossils. He had grown up too poor to go to the local school, but he could read and had a furiously inquisitive mind. He could have read articles about geology in the local newspaper, because the town’s inhabitants were as excited to learn about what was beneath the earth as we are today about explorations in space and the oceans. Besides, mammoth bones had been found in [which states] starting as early as 1705. Just a year before Dexter was born, the famous Charles Willson Peale had excavated a complete mammoth skeleton in a farmer’s field about 100 miles away in eastern New York state.

But what Dexter Marsh saw in the sidewalk slab were not fossilized bones, but footprints that looked exactly like when a turkey steps into mud—but these were in rock! Marsh was mystified. So were his neighbors. One, a young physician named James Deane, thought the discovery so curious that he wrote to two scientists, Edward Hitchcock at Amherst College and Benjamin Silliman at Yale College, to see what they thought.
